# Part 1 — AI Fluency: Working with Claude

> **The most important part of the Claude stack runs on no hardware: it's in your head.** AI
> fluency is the ability to work *with* AI systems effectively, efficiently, ethically, and safely.
> It is what separates someone who occasionally gets lucky with a chatbot from someone who can
> reliably direct an agent to do real work — and know whether it actually did. This Part teaches
> Anthropic's framework for that skill: the **4 D's**.

## Why fluency comes first

You can learn every flag of Claude Code and every field of the Claude API and still produce
garbage — confidently, at scale — if you don't know *what* to ask for, *how* to ask, how to *tell
whether the answer is any good*, and how to stay *accountable* for shipping it. Tools amplify
judgment; they don't replace it. So we start with judgment.

The framework comes from educators **Rick Dakan** and **Joseph Feller** with Anthropic, and it is
refreshingly concrete. Fluency is four practices:

- **Delegation** — deciding what to hand to the AI, what to keep, and how to split the work.
- **Description** — communicating the task so an AI can actually do it.
- **Discernment** — judging the output, the process, and the system's behaviour critically.
- **Diligence** — taking responsibility: honesty, transparency, verification, and safety.

These aren't a checklist you do once; they're a loop you run every time you collaborate.

## A worked example you're already inside

This book is a live demonstration. Another book on this site (*Production Engineering*) was written
and refactored by Claude Code: work was **delegated** to nine subagents in parallel; each got a
precise **description** of the chapter and format to produce; the results were **discerned** by
re-reading them and running their code; and **diligence** meant byte-verifying every runnable
example and formatting every file before it shipped. The final chapter of this Part dissects that
exact workflow through the 4 D's, so the framework lands as something you've *seen*, not just read.
